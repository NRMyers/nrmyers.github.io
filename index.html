My Research Experience

I am a Galactic Archeologist; meaning I study the chemical and dynamical history of the Milky Way. Specifically, I utilize the chemical abundances of open and globular clusters to explore the past and present distribution of different element groups, as well as how that distribution may have evolved throughout the Milky Way’s lifetime. Below are a couple specific projects I have had the opportunity to work on while being a graduate student under Dr. Peter Frinchaboy.

Open Clusters with SDSS/APOGEE

As a part of the Open Cluster Chemical Abundances and Mapping (OCCAM) survey, I built and published the largest (at the time) open cluster catalog with uniform chemical abundances of the Sloan Digital Sky Survey’s APOGEE spectrograph. This catalog included 2061 high-probability stellar members across 153 different open clusters which each had abundances for up to 16 different elements. We were able to use these abundances to determine that the radial abundance gradients may be more uniform through time than originally thought.

For more information see: Myers et al. 2022

Optical Follow-Up

While SDSS/APOGEE provides many high-resolution abundances for light and iron-peak elements, the neutron capture abundances remain elusive. These abundances, created in more energetic processes, such as neutron star mergers, are key to fill in the Galactic chemical evolution picture. I have had the amazing opportunity to travel to multiple observatories, including Keck, Las Companas, McDonald, and Lick Observatories, to observe my own high-resolution (R~60,000), optical, spectra for the stars found in the OCCAM catalog. With these spectra, I will derive by-hand abundances for s- and r-process elements and use them to further explore the evolution of the radial abundance gradients.
