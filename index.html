My Research Experience

I am a Galactic Archeologist; I study the chemical and dynamical history of the Milky Way. Specifically, I utilize the chemical abundances of open and globular clusters to explore the past and present distribution of different element groups, as well as how that distribution may have evolved throughout the Milky Way’s lifetime. Below are a couple specific projects I have had the opportunity to work on while being a graduate student under Dr. Peter Frinchaboy.

Open Clusters with SDSS/APOGEE

As a part of the Open Cluster Chemical Abundances and Mapping (OCCAM) survey, I built and published the largest (at the time) open cluster catalog with uniform chemical abundances of the Sloan Digital Sky Survey’s APOGEE spectrograph. This catalog includes 2061 high-probability stellar members across 153 different open clusters which each have abundances for up to 16 different elements. We use this dataset to analyze the radial abundance gradients in the Milky Way and how they evolve through time.

For more information see: Myers et al. 2022

Optical Follow-Up

While SDSS/APOGEE provides many high-resolution abundances for light and iron-peak elements, the neutron capture abundances remain elusive. I have had the amazing opportunity to travel to multiple observatories and observe my own high-resolution (R~60,000), optical, spectra for the stars found in the OCCAM catalog. I have also taken the dive into spectral analysis so that I can derive my own neutron capture abundances with IRAF and BACCHUS. Finally, I will use these new abundances to explore the evolution of the n-capture radial abundance gradients and calibrate chemical-age relationships (known as chemical clocks).
